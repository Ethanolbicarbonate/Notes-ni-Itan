User Scenarios and Storyboarding

#hci

What is a user scenario?

describes realistic situations relevant to the design of a solution of a persona

Tip

teams can appreciate user interactions in context. That is why this is useful since it helps them understand the practical needs and behaviors of users

User scenarios are not User Stories
User scenarios
  • are short narratives (has plot)
  • dives into motivations, needs, and barriers of users to your design, product, or feature
  • details out how would users likely experience as they use the ideal solution
User stories
  • are short (one sentence long)
  • statements that describe a user's goal (as a [], I want [], so that [])
What do you need to create a user scenario?
Time
  • 1 hr brainstorming session
  • 2-3 hrs reviewing and refining
  • 1 hr prioritizing
  • 1 hr drawing scenario (optional)
Materials
  • Whiteboard and sticky notes
  • Pens
  • online brainstorming tool
  • drawing materials (e.g. paper, pens) (optional)
How do you create a user scenario?
Step 1: Book a meeting with participants who feel they understand your users well

Can be with others at your company or actual users

book as many people as you feel you can guide through a brainstorming session without it getting out of hand
Step 2: Conduct a brainstorming session

Good user scenarios are broken up into 3 distinct parts and brainstorming session should be as well

have your team spend 10 mins. on each part and generate as many ideas as possible

3 Parts of User Scenarios

1: Context and goal
  • who is this person?
  • what skills do they or don't have?
  • what tools are they familliar with?
  • where are they when this scenario takes place?
  • what are their circumstances leading up to this point?
  • what are their goals? why?
2: Problem and current process
  • probelms they currently face and why.
  • their current process/ how they do things currently.
  • their issues with this process.
3: New process
  • steps they will go through with this new process.
  • how does this new process help them achieve their goals or solve their problems.
Step 4: Prioritize them

Prioritize which scenarios best represent your product and business goals
Its unreasonable to use every single scenario as a guide for your product

Get your team involved again so you will come to an agreement as a team on why you are building this in the first place
Step 5: Draw them out (optional)

Turn them into UX storyboards

this hekps communicate your user scenarios to your team
Step 6: Document and share them

Make sure your final, prioritized scenarios are documented and
shared with the rest of your company.

Example of a User Scenario
context and goal

Dave, 67, retired, and with intermediate computer skills, tends to hoard a lot of old junk he’s collected over the years because he keeps saying that he’s going to fix it up or repurpose it. He realizes it’s starting to get out of hand and he’s now determined to tackle some of these projects, starting with his old, broken wall-clock.

problem and current process

The problem is he doesn’t know where to start. He has so many ideas for each project but doesn’t know how they will turn out. He also hasn’t sat down and planned the steps and materials he will need for each project.

He would listen to the suggestions his friends would give him when they came over, then write down some of the better ideas. He would then experiment with his projects for hours without much to show for it, and constantly make trips to the store for new materials as his ideas evolve, however, with no solid plans, his projects often were not finished.

new process

Using e-create, all that has changed. He logs into e-create and types in “broken clock”, then sifts through a few recipes until he finds one called “Stylish picture frame using an old clock”.

He looks at the steps and other materials required and decides to go for it. He prints the recipe, buys the exact materials, and gets to work.

A few hours later, Dave is mounting his new picture frame to his wall. He snaps a photo and submits it back on to e-create with a review of the recipe.

Tips for a great user scenario

Use these scenarios as context for tasks in your usability tests, and at the end of each task, ask your participant if the scenario made sense to them or is something they often go through.

Focus on the user’s goal, not the task. This can help designers open their mind and come up with unique and innovative ideas.

The What, Why, & When of Storyboarding in UX Design

Storyboarding explores scenarios
  • visually
  • creates an experience for the team
  • illustrates expectations
What is a storyboard?
  • a way of graphically organized story - sequence of events presented visually
  • meant to tell a story and illustrate the process and experience of an event
the concept is credited to Walt Disney Studios in 1930s
Storybboard in product design
  • UX storyboard can visually predict and explore user experience and help understand users current motivations and experiences connected to a problem.
  • UX design can start with a template with persona, scenario, and captions
Nielsen Norman Group

The persona or role that corresponds to that scenario is clearly specified at the top of the storyboard

Important aspects of storyboarding
the scene and environment

For example, Tracy in the classroom, morning time, with a few students in the space.

the user persona

Who are they and what are some of their pain points? Include emotions in the process.

the goal

What is the problem they are trying to solve and how do they get there?

the narrative arch

A storyboard is a story — make sure to include a beginning, middle, and end.

When to storyboard?
during the discovery phase of a new product

Storyboarding can summarize and explain existing user stories and problems really well.

Tip

After first user interviews or field research, it can also summarize your findings superbly.

while building the product

model the user interaction with a product based on data gathered from user testing, interviews, or analytics. Here, we can speak of both existing or developing products

Tip

UX storyboard shows their embeddedness in daily situations as well as where people use them

Drawing skills dont matter!
